# Five-Year CAGR: Investment Risk

A five-year Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) measures an investment's average annualized return over a five-year period, assuming returns compound yearly. While it primarily calculates past growth rather than directly measuring risk, investors use it to evaluate risk by contextualizing historical performance and setting realistic expectations.

## Applications in Risk Analysis

### Setting Realistic Baseline Expectations

By calculating the steady rate needed to get from year zero to year five, a 5-year CAGR helps prevent anchoring your expectations to short-term, unsustainable spikes in a stock's price or earnings.

- **Where it's used:** Long-term portfolio planning and goal-setting.
- **Risk context:** It smooths out year-to-year noise, giving investors a grounded view of what a company has actually delivered over time rather than what one exceptional quarter might suggest.

### Evaluating Business Fundamentals

Investors calculate a 5-year revenue and earnings CAGR to spot fundamental business risk.

- **Where it's used:** Comparing a stock's price trajectory against its underlying revenue and earnings growth.
- **Risk context:** If a stock's price is soaring but its fundamental revenue CAGR over five years is stagnant, it may indicate a speculative bubble rather than organic growth.

### Comparing Against Benchmarks

By measuring a stock's 5-year CAGR against the CAGR of a benchmark index (like the S&P 500), investors can identify if the stock is properly compensating them for the risk and volatility they are taking on.

- **Where it's used:** Relative performance evaluation and risk-adjusted return analysis.
- **Risk context:** A stock that underperforms its benchmark CAGR over five years may not be worth the additional company-specific risk compared to a passive index investment.

## Key Risks and Limitations

- **Hides Volatility:** Because CAGR calculates a "smoothed" constant rate, it hides volatility—meaning a stock with an erratic path will have the exact same CAGR as a stock with slow and steady growth.
- **Backward-Looking:** Past CAGR does not predict future returns; market conditions, competitive dynamics, and macroeconomic shifts can dramatically alter growth trajectories.
- **Window Sensitivity:** The specific five-year window chosen can significantly impact the result, especially if it begins or ends during unusual market conditions.

## Contextual Usage

- **Growth Stocks:** Useful for verifying whether rapid price appreciation is backed by real compounding in revenue or earnings.
- **Income Stocks:** Helps assess whether dividend growth has compounded at a rate that keeps pace with inflation and market returns.
- **Complementary Metrics:** Best used alongside volatility measures (standard deviation, beta), PEG ratio, and earnings quality indicators to form a complete risk picture.
