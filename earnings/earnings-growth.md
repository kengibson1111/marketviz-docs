# Five-Year Earnings Growth: Investment Risk

Five-year earnings growth is primarily used to calculate the PEG (Price/Earnings-to-Growth) ratio and to assess a company's momentum sustainability and historical consistency.

## Questions This Answers

- Is this stock's high valuation justified by its actual earnings growth track record?
- Has this company consistently grown profits, or was growth concentrated in one or two lucky years?
- Is the current price-to-earnings multiple stretched far above the historical norm?
- Can I trust that this company is a long-term compounder rather than a cyclical fluke?

## Applications in Risk Analysis

### Calculating the PEG Ratio

The [PEG ratio](../financial/peg.md) modifies the traditional P/E ratio by factoring in the rate of earnings growth. It is calculated as: P/E Ratio divided by Earnings Growth Rate.

- **Where it's used:** Investors use the projected or historical 5-year compound annual growth rate (CAGR) as the denominator.
- **Risk context:** If a stock has a high P/E ratio (making it look expensive), but a strong 5-year growth rate, its PEG ratio might show it is actually undervalued for the growth it provides. Conversely, a high P/E without proportional 5-year growth highlights the risk of an overvalued, speculative stock.

### Judging Valuation Premiums

Comparing a stock's earnings growth multiple against its 5-year average multiple (often its 5-year average P/E ratio) helps mitigate valuation risk.

- **Where it's used:** Comparing Next-Twelve-Month (NTM) P/E to the 5-year historical average.
- **Risk context:** If the current multiple is far above the 5-year average, the market is pricing in near-perfect execution. If growth stumbles, the stock risks a sharp price correction as its multiple reverts to the historical mean.

### Historical Consistency & Capital Appreciation

Analyzing past 5-year historical earnings (EPS) growth is a core tenet of growth investing.

- **Where it's used:** Evaluating the compound annual growth rate (CAGR) over the previous 3 to 5 years.
- **Risk context:** It measures business resilience. Stocks that consistently grow earnings year-over-year carry less fundamental risk than volatile companies, helping investors confidently identify long-term compounders.

## Key Risks and Limitations

- **Backward-Looking Bias:** Historical growth does not guarantee future performance; market conditions, competition, and management changes can disrupt trends.
  - *How to address:* Cross-reference historical earnings growth with forward analyst [EPS estimates](eps-projections.md) and [revenue growth](revenue-growth.md) trends. If forward projections diverge sharply from the historical rate, investigate why — a maturing market or leadership change may explain the gap.
- **Earnings Manipulation:** Companies may use accounting techniques to inflate short-term EPS growth, masking underlying weakness.
  - *How to address:* Check the company's [earnings quality](earnings-quality.md) score and run it through [manipulation detection tools](earnings-manipulation-detection.md) like the Beneish M-Score or Sloan Accrual Ratio. Also compare reported EPS growth to free cash flow growth — if cash flow is flat while EPS is rising, the growth may be accounting-driven rather than operational.
- **Cyclicality:** Some industries naturally experience boom-and-bust cycles, making a 5-year window potentially misleading if it captures only one phase.
  - *How to address:* Extend the lookback period to 7–10 years if data is available, or compare against a full economic cycle. Also check revenue growth alongside earnings growth — revenue that grew steadily while earnings spiked suggests the profit surge is cyclical margin expansion rather than structural improvement.

## Contextual Usage

- **Growth Stocks:** Essential for evaluating whether a premium valuation is justified by sustained earnings expansion.
- **Value Stocks:** Useful for identifying turnaround candidates showing improving earnings trajectories.
- **Complementary Metrics:** Best used alongside PEG ratio, [free cash flow](../financial/free-cash-flow.md), and revenue growth to build a complete picture of fundamental risk.
