# Price-to-Book Ratio: Investment Risk

Investing in a stock with a high Price-to-Book (P/B) ratio carries elevated risk because the stock is trading at a premium to its physical assets. It means the market has high expectations for future growth, leaving little margin for error; any shortfall in performance can cause the stock price to drop significantly.

## Questions This Answers

- How much am I paying above the company's actual asset value?
- If this company failed today, how big is the gap between what I paid and what assets are worth?
- Is a high P/B justified by strong profitability, or am I overpaying for hype?
- Is the low P/B a genuine bargain, or does it reflect a company with worthless assets?

## Key Risk Factors

- **Overvaluation:** A high P/B (e.g., above 3.0 or 4.0) usually suggests a stock is overpriced relative to the equity recorded on its balance sheet.
- **Low Margin of Error:** When you pay a steep premium, even minor earnings misses or negative market sentiment can trigger sharp price corrections.
- **Downside Risk during Liquidations:** If the company fails, book value represents the theoretical amount shareholders would receive if all assets were sold. A high P/B means that in a worst-case scenario, the gap between what you paid and what the assets are actually worth in a fire sale is massive.

## When a High P/B is "Acceptable"

### Service and Tech Companies

Many modern companies rely on intellectual property, brand value, or massive user bases rather than physical property. These intangible assets do not show up well on book value, artificially inflating the P/B ratio.

### High Profitability

A high P/B is justified if the company is generating massive returns on its equity (ROE).
