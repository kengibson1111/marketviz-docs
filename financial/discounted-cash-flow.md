# Discounted Cash Flow: Investment Risk

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) modeling is used to estimate a stock's intrinsic value by projecting its future free cash flows and discounting them back to the present. It helps investors analyze risk by answering two core questions: Is the current price fundamentally justified? And what assumptions are required to break even?

## Key Areas Where DCF Modeling Targets Investing Risk

### 1. Intrinsic Value vs. Market Price

By calculating exactly what a stock is worth based on its cash generation, you identify the "margin of safety" — the gap between the stock's intrinsic value and its current trading price. A wide margin of safety means the stock trades well below its calculated worth, offering a buffer against estimation errors or economic downturns.

### 2. Assessing the Discount Rate (Cost of Equity)

The discount rate in DCF adjusts cash flows for the time value of money and inherent risk. Stocks with higher volatility or debt use a higher discount rate, making future cash flows less valuable in today's terms and automatically lowering the stock's justified price. The higher the discount rate you need to apply, the riskier the investment.

### 3. Sensitivity Analysis

The most powerful risk-management aspect of DCF modeling is "what-if" testing. Investors test how a stock's valuation shifts if:

- Future growth rates slow down
- The discount rate (representing market risk and interest rates) rises
- Revenue assumptions prove overly optimistic

This reveals how fragile an investment is to changing economic conditions. A stock whose valuation collapses under minor assumption changes carries significantly more risk.

### 4. Accounting for Cash, Not Accounting Metrics

By focusing purely on free cash flow rather than accounting earnings, DCF removes the ability of inflated net incomes or accounting maneuvers to hide underlying operational or liquidity risks. This gives investors a clearer view of actual business performance.
