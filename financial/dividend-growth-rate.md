# Five-Year Dividend Growth Rate: Investment Risk

The five-year dividend growth rate is used to analyze investing risk by evaluating a company's financial stability, inflation-adjusted total returns, and management's commitment to shareholders. It acts as an indicator of corporate maturity and protects against "yield traps".

## Dividend Discount Models (DDM)

The metric is used as a primary input in the Gordon Growth Model (GGM) to calculate a stock's intrinsic value, helping investors determine if the stock is overvalued or undervalued.

- **The Risk Signal:** A declining 5-year dividend growth rate lowers the intrinsic value estimate, suggesting the stock may be overpriced relative to its future cash flows.
- **How It's Used:** Investors plug the historical growth rate into the [Gordon Growth Model](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/g/gordongrowthmodel.asp) to set a fair-value target. A shrinking rate signals that expected returns are falling, increasing the risk of overpaying for the stock.

## Inflation Risk Assessment

High historical dividend growth helps preserve purchasing power. If a stock's dividend grows faster than the rate of inflation, it limits real-dollar value loss for income-focused investors.

- **The Risk Signal:** A 5-year growth rate that trails inflation means the investor's income stream is losing real value over time, even if the nominal payout stays flat or rises slightly.
- **How It's Used:** Investors compare the growth rate against the average inflation rate to ensure their dividend income maintains or increases its purchasing power.

## Evaluating Financial Health

A steady, upward-trending 5-year growth rate suggests the underlying earnings are also growing, demonstrating that the payout is funded by business growth rather than debt.

- **The Risk Signal:** An erratic or declining growth rate may indicate deteriorating fundamentals—shrinking margins, rising debt, or unsustainable payout ratios.
- **How It's Used:** Analysts look for consistent year-over-year increases as evidence that the company generates enough free cash flow to support and grow its dividend without financial strain.

## Management Commitment

Consistently rising dividends over a 5-year period—especially through varying economic cycles—indicates strong capital allocation, signaling a lower-risk business model.

- **The Risk Signal:** A company that freezes or cuts its dividend during mild downturns may lack the financial resilience or management discipline that long-term income investors require.
- **How It's Used:** Investors use the 5-year track record to distinguish companies with durable competitive advantages from those whose payouts depend on favorable market conditions.

## Screening and Indexing

Platforms and index funds use the 5-year dividend growth rate as a strict requirement to filter out volatile companies and highlight "quality" investments.

- **The Risk Signal:** Stocks that fail to meet a minimum growth threshold are excluded from dividend-focused indices, which can reduce institutional demand and liquidity.
- **How It's Used:** Tools like [Schwab's dividend stock screener](https://www.schwab.com/learn/story/ways-to-evaluate-dividend-growing-stocks) and indices like the Dow Jones U.S. Dividend 100 Index require a positive 5-year growth rate, helping investors avoid companies with unstable or declining payouts.
